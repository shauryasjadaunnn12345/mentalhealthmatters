What is Insomnia?

Insomnia is a common sleep disorder characterized by difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking up too early and not being able to return to sleep. It can be acute (short-term) or chronic (long-lasting), and it can significantly impact your quality of life, energy levels, mood, and overall health.

Common Symptoms

  • Difficulty falling asleep at night
  • Waking up during the night
  • Waking up too early
  • Not feeling well-rested after sleep
  • Daytime tiredness or sleepiness
  • Irritability, depression or anxiety
  • Difficulty paying attention or focusing
  • Increased errors or accidents

Types of Insomnia

  • Acute Insomnia: Brief and often happens because of life circumstances (stress, traumatic event). Lasts for days or weeks.
  • Chronic Insomnia: Disrupted sleep that occurs at least three nights per week and lasts for at least three months.
  • Onset Insomnia: Difficulty falling asleep at the beginning of the night.
  • Maintenance Insomnia: Inability to stay asleep, waking up during the night.
  • Comorbid Insomnia: Occurs with another condition like depression, anxiety, or chronic pain.

Causes and Risk Factors

Insomnia can be caused by a variety of factors, including:

Lifestyle Factors

  • Irregular sleep schedule
  • Napping too much during the day
  • Lack of physical activity
  • Consuming caffeine or nicotine close to bedtime
  • Drinking alcohol before bed
  • Eating late in the evening
  • Using electronic devices before bed

Medical and Psychological Factors

  • Stress and anxiety
  • Depression
  • Chronic pain
  • Medications that interfere with sleep
  • Medical conditions (asthma, arthritis, etc.)
  • Neurological disorders
  • Hormonal changes

Understanding Your Results

Your insomnia test results will provide an indication of the severity of your sleep difficulties:

  • No clinically significant insomnia (0-7): Your responses suggest minimal or no insomnia symptoms.
  • Subthreshold insomnia (8-14): You may be experiencing some sleep difficulties that don't significantly impact your daily life.
  • Clinical insomnia (moderate, 15-21): Your sleep difficulties are causing noticeable distress and impairment in daily activities.
  • Clinical insomnia (severe, 22-28): Your sleep difficulties are causing significant distress and impairment in multiple areas of your life.

Important: This test is a screening tool, not a diagnostic instrument. A formal diagnosis can only be made by a qualified healthcare professional through comprehensive evaluation.
