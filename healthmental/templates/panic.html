What is Panic Disorder?

Panic Disorder is an anxiety disorder characterized by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks. A panic attack is a sudden episode of intense fear that triggers severe physical reactions when there is no real danger or apparent cause. People with panic disorder often worry about when their next attack will occur and actively try to prevent future attacks by avoiding places, situations, or behaviors they associate with panic attacks.

Panic attacks typically peak within 10 minutes and subside within 20-30 minutes, but the fear of having another attack can persist and significantly impact daily life.

Common Symptoms of Panic Disorder

During a panic attack, you may experience:

Physical Symptoms

  • Rapid heart rate or palpitations
  • Sweating or chills
  • Trembling or shaking
  • Shortness of breath or feeling of smothering
  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Nausea or abdominal distress
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Numbness or tingling sensations

Emotional & Cognitive Symptoms

  • Intense fear of dying or losing control
  • Feelings of unreality or detachment (derealization)
  • Feeling detached from oneself (depersonalization)
  • Fear of "going crazy"
  • Intense need to escape
  • Anticipatory anxiety between attacks
  • Avoidance of situations where attacks occurred

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of panic disorder is not fully understood, but several factors may contribute:

  • Genetics: Panic disorder often runs in families, suggesting a genetic predisposition.
  • Brain Chemistry: Imbalances in neurotransmitters like serotonin, norepinephrine, and GABA may play a role.
  • Temperament: People who are more sensitive to stress or prone to negative emotions may be at higher risk.
  • Life Experiences: Traumatic events, major life stress, or childhood adversity can trigger panic disorder.
  • Other Mental Health Conditions: Having other anxiety disorders, depression, or substance use disorders increases risk.

Treatment Options

Panic disorder is highly treatable. Common approaches include:

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Helps identify and change thought patterns and behaviors that trigger panic attacks.
  • Panic-Focused Psychodynamic Psychotherapy (PFPP): Explores emotional conflicts that may contribute to panic symptoms.
  • Exposure Therapy: Gradually exposes you to feared situations in a safe environment to reduce fear responses.

Medication

  • SSRIs and SNRIs: First-line medications that help regulate serotonin and norepinephrine.
  • Benzodiazepines: Fast-acting medications for acute panic symptoms (used short-term due to dependency risk).
  • Beta-Blockers: Help control physical symptoms like rapid heart rate and trembling.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this test suitable for teenagers?
Yes, this test is appropriate for teens 13 years and older. For younger children, we recommend consulting a pediatrician or child psychologist.

How accurate is this panic disorder test?
This test is based on the Panic Disorder Severity Scale (PDSS), which has been validated in clinical settings. While it's a reliable screening tool, it's not a substitute for professional diagnosis.

What's the difference between panic attacks and anxiety attacks?
Panic attacks are sudden, intense episodes of fear that peak within minutes, while anxiety attacks typically build up gradually in response to a specific stressor and last longer.

Can panic disorder be cured?
While there's no absolute "cure" for panic disorder, it's highly treatable. With proper treatment, most people experience significant reduction in symptoms and can lead fulfilling lives.

Should I go to the emergency room during a panic attack?
If you're experiencing chest pain, difficulty breathing, or other symptoms that could indicate a medical emergency, it's best to seek immediate medical care, especially if it's your first panic attack.
